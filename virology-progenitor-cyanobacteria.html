Progenitor virus and the cyanobacteria — origin question

Every RE virus traces back to the same source: a flower in Africa that grew in symbiosis with a unique cyanobacterium. The Progenitor lineage is RE's creation myth. RE9 finally explains the cyanobacterial side, and it changes how I read the whole series.

My take:Progenitor was the most under-explained piece of RE lore for two decades. RE9's codex changes the franchise's biological foundation. Few players noticed.

Why the cyanobacterium matters

Pre-RE9, Progenitor was canonized as a flower-borne virus discovered by Lord Spencer's team in the 1960s. The biological detail stopped at "rare flower". RE9's Vivien Davies codex finally specifies the second half — the flower grew in tight symbiosis with a cyanobacterium that supplied the virus's unique surface protein.

That changes everything. It means Progenitor isn't a virus in the strict sense. It's a cyanobacteria-derived bioactive molecule that uses viral delivery machinery. The Stairway gene segment from the cyanobacterium is what makes Progenitor work across species.

Real-world parallel: cyanobacteria as Earth's oldest engineers

Cyanobacteria are 3.5 billion years old. They invented oxygen-producing photosynthesis. Real cyanobacteria absolutely do produce bioactive molecules that affect higher organisms — microcystins are real toxins that real cyanobacteria make, with effects on liver and nervous system. So the "cyanobacterium produces a thing that affects animal biology" premise is biologically reasonable.

What Capcom adds: a specific cyanobacterium that produces a bioactive RNA capable of cross-kingdom transfer through retroviral-style integration. That's not real. But the inspiration is.

What this retcon does to series biology

Every RE virus is now traceable to one organism. T-virus, G-virus, T-Veronica, T-Abyss, C-virus, E-pathogen — all use the Progenitor backbone with different modifications. That gives RE biology a shared origin instead of independent virus families.

I like this for two reasons. It explains why every RE virus produces broadly similar effects (mutation, mental degradation, regenerative tissue). And it sets up a real reason for the Spencer dynasty's obsession — they were never just chasing immortality, they were chasing the only universal genetic delivery system on Earth.

What doesn't work biologically

The "single ancestral cyanobacterium" framing is a stretch even by RE standards. Cyanobacteria reproduce by binary fission — there are millions of genetically related lineages in any sample of pond water. Capcom's lore says only one specific symbiosis (the Sussex Tower flower) produced the Progenitor-compatible cyanobacterium. That's not how bacterial evolution works.

The handwave: "extremely rare combination of mutations" produced this single lineage. Acceptable for fiction. Annoying for anyone who actually studied microbiology.

Why RE9 introduced this now

My read: Capcom is unifying the franchise lore for the next generation. RE10 (assumed) needs a clean biology foundation if it's going to handle the Wesker / Spencer endgame. The cyanobacterial origin gives all future RE games a single creation point to reference back to.

Korean fan theory posts on RE9 lore agree on this. The Naver consensus is that the cyanobacterium retcon is setup for the BSAA endgame arc in RE10. I think that's correct.

Where I want more detail

RE9 codex doesn't explain what happened to the original Sussex Tower flower or the original cyanobacterium colony. If Capcom kept both alive somewhere (an in-universe sample), it's a future plot device. If they're both extinct, the surviving Progenitor strains in BSAA labs are all derived from samples — which means catastrophic ecological risk if a lab loses containment.

Either answer is interesting. Capcom should pick one.

Verdict

This is the most foundational lore change RE9 introduced. Most reviewers missed it because the codex is dense and the cyanobacterium isn't mentioned in main story dialogue. If you care about RE biology, read the Vivien Davies background terminals in chapter 3 carefully. The cyanobacterium reference is buried but it's there.

Strong pick of the week for lore enthusiasts.
