RE9 vs RE2 Remake — which one should you play first (complete 2026 comparison)

Resident Evil: Requiem (2026) and Resident Evil 2 Remake (2019) are the two most-recommended modern entries for newcomers. This long-form comparison covers gameplay, narrative weight, technical fidelity, replay value, accessibility, and Korean-community reception across both titles. The verdict is not what most listicles claim.

My take:RE2 Remake is the better first RE for most players. RE9 is the better third or fourth RE. The order matters more than the games.

The 90-second verdict

If you have not played any Resident Evil game and you want to start with the modern Capcom RE style, start with RE2 Remake. Its difficulty curve is more forgiving, its narrative is self-contained, its protagonist roster is smaller, and its hard-built tutorial pacing teaches RE's combat language better than any other entry. RE9 expects you to know that language; it does not teach it from scratch.

If you have already played at least one modern RE — anything from RE2 Remake forward — RE9 is a stronger sustained experience. Its boss design is the franchise's best in a decade, its character writing is the most ethically nuanced in the series's history, and its lore payoff in chapters 7 and 8 rewards players who have followed the franchise's institutional plot since 1998. Players who come to RE9 cold often report missing 30-40% of what makes the game distinctive.

Difficulty and combat curve compared

RE2 Remake's Standard difficulty was tuned for a player who has never played a horror-shooter. Aim assist is generous on Standard. Enemy health pools are forgiving. Ammunition drops are calibrated to allow players who waste shots to recover. The Mr. X chase sequences scale to player skill in subtle ways most players never notice — his patrol patterns are easier on lower difficulties without making this fact explicit in-game.

RE9's Standard difficulty assumes baseline combat literacy. The Mortal Edge parry system is taught in a four-second tutorial sequence that most players miss on first play. Enemy AI is more aggressive. Ammunition drops are calibrated for players who use the knife alongside firearms — if you only use firearms, you run out by chapter 5. The Hardcore and Insanity difficulties in RE9 are roughly equivalent to RE2 Remake's Hardcore in raw enemy aggression but require additional system mastery to survive.

Practical recommendation: if RE9 is your first RE, play on Casual. Then play RE2 Remake on Standard. Then return to RE9 on Standard. This unconventional order gives you the cleanest learning curve.

Narrative independence — what you can play standalone

RE2 Remake stands almost entirely alone. Players who have never seen any Resident Evil content can complete RE2 Remake and understand 95% of its narrative. The references to events before September 1998 are sparse, atmospheric rather than load-bearing, and never required for plot comprehension. The chapter 6 sewer sequence's connection to William Birkin's Umbrella research is the only meaningful prior-knowledge moment, and it works fine as fresh worldbuilding for newcomers.

RE9 sits in a more loaded canonical position. The chapter 6 Raccoon City Police Department return only lands fully for players who experienced RE2 Remake's RPD setting. The chapter 7 Mia Winters cameo requires RE7 context to land emotionally. The Project W reveals in chapter 7's ARK facility require RE5 background to feel meaningful. Players who come to RE9 without prior RE experience report enjoying the game but feeling like they were watching the second half of a long conversation without having heard the first half.

This is the actual case for playing RE2 Remake first. Not difficulty, not graphics — narrative groundwork.

The Mr. X comparison — same character, different design intent

Mr. X (T-103 Tyrant unit) appears in both games, and RE9's chapter 6 deployment is the franchise's most direct deliberate callback to RE2 Remake. Reviewers who played both games consistently rank the RE2 Remake Mr. X chase as the more terrifying experience and the RE9 chapter 6 Mr. X chase as the more tactically interesting experience.

RE2 Remake's Mr. X creates dread through unpredictability. His patrol patterns adapt to player position. The audio design — the now-iconic footstep audio — provides spatial information but not always reliable directional cues. Players experience genuine fear because they cannot fully model his behavior.

RE9's chapter 6 Mr. X is more predictable but more deadly. His patrol patterns are fixed across difficulty levels. The audio signature has been faithfully recreated (per Capcom audio direction statements at GDC 2026). However, his attack damage on Insanity is calibrated to one-shot players at default health, removing the survival margin RE2 Remake allowed. The fear shifts from "what is he doing" to "how do I avoid him for the next four minutes."

Which is better is genuinely subjective. Players who prefer atmospheric horror prefer RE2 Remake's version. Players who prefer tactical horror prefer RE9's version.

Technical fidelity and performance

RE2 Remake on PS5 and Xbox Series X runs at 60fps with HDR support and ray-traced reflections (added in the 2023 next-gen update). On PC, it scales to 144fps with mod support that the community has extended substantially. The 2019 visual baseline has aged well; the RE Engine's lighting model holds up to current-generation visual expectations.

RE9 launched in February 2026 with the most recent RE Engine iteration. PS5 Pro and Xbox Series X support 60fps with full ray tracing and DLSS-equivalent upscaling. PC implementation supports 240fps with the latest hardware. The visual fidelity advantage over RE2 Remake is real but not dramatic — Capcom's RE Engine produces visually consistent results across generations.

Both games hold up technically. Neither is obviously dated. The choice between them should not be made on technical grounds for any player above mid-range hardware.

Replay value and post-game content

RE2 Remake's New Game Plus carries weapons and inventory upgrades but resets story choices. The Leon-Claire dual protagonist structure provides genuine second-playthrough variation; their stories overlap thematically but unlock different content. The 4th Survivor (Hunk) and Tofu modes add post-campaign arcade-style challenges. Speedrun categories are well-established and competitive globally.

RE9's NG+ inherits weapons, Charms, and currency, and dialogue branches produce eight distinct endings. The true ending unlock conditions reward careful play across multiple chapters. Post-credits content sets up RE10's protagonist directly. Speedrun categories are nascent but competitive. The Mr. Raccoon and trophy completion grind is more demanding than RE2 Remake's equivalent.

Replay value goes to RE9 for sustained engagement, RE2 Remake for arcade replayability.

Korean community reception comparison

RE2 Remake was the highest-selling RE entry in Korea in its launch year. Korean reviewers consistently rank it in the franchise top three. The Korean dub quality was praised at launch and has aged well. The Naver Cafe and DCInside RE communities still maintain active RE2 Remake discussion threads in 2026.

RE9's Korean reception has been more polarized at launch. Reviewers ranked it highly for narrative innovation but criticized chapter 4 pacing and the Rhodes Hill quartz puzzle. The Korean BSAA field commander reveal in the post-credits scene generated the largest single Korean community reaction to any RE9 content. Long-term Korean reception is still settling four months post-launch.

For Korean players specifically, the case for RE9 includes the post-credits Korean protagonist reveal as direct community investment. Korean players who care about representation in mainline RE protagonists have meaningful reason to engage with RE9 immediately.

Accessibility and onboarding

RE2 Remake's accessibility options at launch were minimal but were expanded in subsequent patches. The 2023 next-gen update added difficulty option presets, subtitle customization, and combat assist features. The game can now be played with substantial accessibility accommodations for motor, visual, and auditory limitations.

RE9 launched with substantially better accessibility support out of the box. Photosensitivity safety mode, audio cue customization for parry timing, full controller remapping, and visual contrast options were all available at launch. The accessibility implementation is the franchise's best to date.

For players who require accessibility accommodations, RE9 is the stronger choice. For players who don't require accommodations, both work.

Price and value

RE2 Remake in 2026 is regularly available below 20 USD on sale. It's a seven-year-old release with extensive discount history. Full price purchase is rarely necessary.

RE9 in 2026 remains at full price — 60 USD standard, 70 USD deluxe — with first significant discounts expected in summer 2026 sales cycles. Patient buyers will see RE9 at 25-30 USD by late 2026.

Value per dollar on current pricing strongly favors RE2 Remake. Players who can wait six months to a year for RE9 sales get better value either way.

The hidden third option — play both in deliberate order

The strongest play pattern, for newcomers with time and budget for two games, is RE2 Remake first, then RE9 second, with at least a one-week gap between. The gap matters because RE2 Remake teaches the franchise's combat language, but RE9's narrative payoffs land harder if you played RE2 Remake recently enough to recognize the RPD callbacks.

This order also serves Korean community engagement well. RE2 Remake's Korean community is mature and welcoming to newcomers. RE9's Korean community is currently most engaged with the post-credits Korean protagonist reveal and welcomes new players who arrive with prior RE knowledge.

Common mistakes people make in this comparison

The first mistake is treating this as a quality comparison. Both games are well-made. The decision is not about quality — it's about player context and prior knowledge.

The second mistake is assuming difficulty equals depth. RE9 is harder for newcomers, but harder doesn't mean better-designed. RE2 Remake's lower friction onboarding is intentional design, not weakness.

The third mistake is ignoring the price gap. A newcomer comparing 20 USD to 60 USD should not be making a quality comparison; they should be calibrating risk. Try the cheaper one first.

What this comparison cannot tell you

This comparison can't predict your personal reaction to either game. Some players prefer RE9's tactical horror; some prefer RE2 Remake's atmospheric dread. Some prefer Grace Ashcroft's investigator-protagonist framing; some prefer Leon Kennedy's rookie-cop framing. Personal taste matters.

If you have access to demos for either, play them. RE9's demo (released January 2026) covers chapter 1; RE2 Remake's "1-Shot Demo" was discontinued but the first 30 minutes of the game are representative.

Final recommendation by reader type

First-time RE player, modern preferences: RE2 Remake first. RE9 after a one-week break.

Returning RE veteran, played RE2 Remake recently: RE9. Skip the comparison and dive in.

Korean community member who cares about Korean protagonist representation: RE9 immediately, with the post-credits scene as motivation.

Budget-constrained player who can buy only one: RE2 Remake. Better cost-to-experience ratio, more polished, equally satisfying.

Speedrun-curious player: RE2 Remake. Established competitive scene, mature route knowledge, no early-stage instability.

Lore enthusiast: Both, in order. RE2 Remake first. RE9 specifically for chapter 7 ARK facility and chapter 8 brother reveal.

Closing thought

The franchise has been alive for 28 years. Both of these games sit in the strongest creative period in RE's history. Capcom's RE Engine has paid out in two consecutive major entries that are each defensibly the best modern entry in different ways. Whichever you play first, you will not waste your time. The order is the question — not the choice.
